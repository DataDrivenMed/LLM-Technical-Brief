Clinical LLM Benchmarks: Article, Codebase, and Dataset Insight Report

Prepared: 2026-06-12

This report analyzes the Nature Medicine article "General-purpose large language models outperform specialized clinical AI tools on medical benchmarks," the public codebase at https://github.com/nyuolab/clinical-llm-benchmarks, and the two public benchmark datasets referenced by the article:

  • MedQA-USMLE-4-options: https://huggingface.co/datasets/GBaker/MedQA-USMLE-4-options
  • HealthBench: https://huggingface.co/datasets/openai/healthbench

The Nature article page used as the source of truth is:

https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04431-5

This is an audit and explanation report. It does not rerun model generations, call paid APIs, query clinical tools, or reproduce manuscript statistics from scratch.

Executive Summary

The article evaluates whether specialized clinical AI tools actually outperform frontier general-purpose large language models in medical tasks. The study compares OpenEvidence and UpToDate Expert AI against GPT-5.2, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview, Claude Opus 4.6, and, for the real clinical query benchmark, Google Search AI Overview. It uses three evaluation layers: MedQA for multiple-choice medical knowledge, HealthBench for rubric-based health response quality, and a private Real Clinical Queries benchmark built from 100 de-identified physician queries.

The central finding is straightforward: in this study, general-purpose frontier LLMs outperform the specialized clinical tools across all evaluated stages. This does not mean frontier LLMs are automatically safe for independent clinical deployment. It means that, under these benchmark designs, the commercial clinical tools did not show the expected performance advantage over frontier models, despite their clinical branding and presumed domain-specific retrieval or tuning.

The codebase is best understood as two connected systems:

  1. A Python benchmark pipeline for preparing HealthBench samples, generating or importing model answers, scoring MedQA, grading HealthBench with an LLM judge panel, and organizing a run matrix.
  2. A Next.js and Postgres blinded clinician review platform for turning a local matrix of model responses into anonymized rater tasks, collecting clinician ratings, managing assignments, and exporting results.

The public repository supports a substantial part of the article's workflow, but it is not a complete one-command reproduction package. The biggest reproducibility gaps are the private RCQ dataset, manually collected browser outputs for OpenEvidence, UpToDate Expert AI, and Google AI Overview, the exact historical model behavior of February 2026 frontier systems, the MedQA preparation step from raw Hugging Face rows into the pipeline's expected JSONL format, and the manuscript-level statistical analysis scripts.

For non-technical readers, the takeaway is: the paper is less about whether any single chatbot is "good" or "bad" and more about independent verification. It shows that health systems should not assume a tool is superior because it is marketed for clinical use. Procurement and governance should require benchmark evidence, local validation, clinician review, safety review, and monitoring.

For technical readers, the strongest part of the repository is its explicit separation of model generation/import, benchmark scoring, and blinded human evaluation. The weakest parts are around complete reproducibility: missing raw response artifacts, manual collection paths, a likely missing MedQA transformation script, reliance on LLM-as-judge scoring defaults, and incomplete admin authentication coverage in the clinician review app as inspected.

Hype Versus Evidence Memo

The social-media version of this paper is likely to become: "Frontier LLMs beat clinical AI tools." That is directionally true, but incomplete. The more evidence-faithful version is: under this paper's three benchmark settings, frontier LLMs outperformed OpenEvidence and UpToDate Expert AI, while the strongest real-world evidence came from a private clinician-query benchmark that the public cannot independently inspect.

The paper is meaningful because it tests clinical AI tools head-to-head rather than accepting clinical branding as proof. It is also limited because the most important benchmark, RCQ, cannot be released; clinical tool outputs were manually collected through browser interfaces; and the public codebase is supporting infrastructure, not a one-command reproduction package.

My read: this is not empty hype. The article asks the right question and provides real evidence. But the public materials support an audit of the workflow more strongly than they support full independent reproduction of the headline numbers.

Source Inventory

Article

The Nature article is a Nature Medicine Brief Communication available at https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04431-5. The article states that the evaluation used:

  • 500 randomly sampled MedQA questions.
  • 500 randomly sampled HealthBench single-turn prompts.
  • 100 de-identified real clinical queries from a HIPAA-compliant NYU Langone GPT instance.
  • Three frontier LLMs: GPT-5.2, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview, and Claude Opus 4.6.
  • Two specialized clinical AI tools: OpenEvidence and UpToDate Expert AI.
  • Google Search AI Overview for the RCQ stage.
  • Twelve blinded US clinician raters for RCQ.
  • Three independent ratings per question-model response pair.
  • 1,800 initial model-question annotations, reduced after 32 refusals were excluded.

The article's Methods section says the public MedQA and HealthBench subsets can be reproduced with seed 62, while the RCQ dataset cannot be publicly released because it derives from clinical environment queries under institutional review and data use constraints.

What The Paper Actually Tested

The Question

The paper asks a practical question: if a clinician or health system pays for, adopts, or trusts a specialized clinical AI tool, is that tool actually better than a frontier general-purpose LLM?

The implied market assumption is that clinical tools should be better because they may use medical retrieval, domain-specific prompts, curated sources, clinical workflows, or domain-specific tuning. The paper tests that assumption empirically.

The Model Groups

The study separates models into three groups:

  • Frontier API LLMs: GPT-5.2, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview, Claude Opus 4.6.
  • Clinical AI tools: OpenEvidence and UpToDate Expert AI.
  • Search-embedded control for RCQ: Google Search AI Overview.

That grouping matters. GPT, Gemini, and Claude were accessed through APIs. OpenEvidence, UpToDate Expert AI, and Google AI Overview were accessed through browser interfaces. The article explicitly states that clinical tools lack public APIs, which forced browser-based collection and introduced limitations around hidden prompts, retrieval behavior, and formatting.

The Three Benchmark Layers

The article uses a layered design:

  1. MedQA: multiple-choice medical knowledge.
  2. HealthBench: open-ended health response quality, graded against rubrics.
  3. RCQ: real de-identified clinical queries, judged by blinded clinicians.

This is a strong design choice because each layer tests something different. MedQA tests whether a model can select an answer on exam-style questions. HealthBench tests whether a response satisfies clinically meaningful rubric items. RCQ tests whether clinicians find model answers useful, safe, complete, and clear in a realistic setting.

The paper treats RCQ as the strongest evidence because it is independent, real-world, and not likely to have been seen in training. HealthBench is useful but less independent because it is an OpenAI-developed benchmark and uses LLM-based grading.

Main Reported Results

The article reports the following headline results.

MedQA accuracy:

  • Gemini 3.1 Pro: 97.4%
  • GPT-5.2: 94.2%
  • Claude Opus 4.6: 90.2%
  • OpenEvidence: 89.6%
  • UpToDate Expert AI: 88.4%

HealthBench score:

  • GPT-5.2: 88.0
  • Gemini 3.1 Pro: 79.3
  • Claude Opus 4.6: 77.0
  • OpenEvidence: 62.6
  • UpToDate Expert AI: 61.3

RCQ mean aggregate clinician rating:

  • Gemini 3.1 Pro: 3.62
  • GPT-5.2: 3.54
  • Claude Opus 4.6: 3.52
  • Google AI Overview: 3.27
  • OpenEvidence: 3.24
  • UpToDate Expert AI: 3.17

The paper also reports that UpToDate Expert AI refused 19% of RCQ questions, higher than most other models. Safety and hallucination flags did not differ significantly across models in the RCQ analysis, but qualitative error notes were more common for OpenEvidence and Google AI Overview.

What The Result Does Not Prove

The paper does not prove that frontier LLMs are clinically safe as standalone medical systems. It does not prove that specialized clinical tools are never useful. It does not evaluate all possible medical tasks, all subspecialties, all workflows, all hospitals, or all patient-safety contexts.

The study is best read as a snapshot of performance under a specific, well-described evaluation design. The authors themselves emphasize that the landscape changes quickly, that clinical tools were queried through browsers, that benchmarks may have contamination, and that latency and citation quality were not evaluated.

For health systems, the practical conclusion is governance-oriented: do not assume clinical branding equals clinical superiority. Require independent evaluation.

End-To-End Workflow

A practical conceptual workflow looks like this:

Public benchmark data
  -> sampled/prepared JSONL
  -> frontier model API generations
  -> manual/imported clinical tool outputs
  -> raw per-model JSONL files
  -> MedQA extraction/scoring
  -> HealthBench rubric scoring
  -> summary outputs and run metadata

Real clinical query matrix
  -> CSV/XLSX with one query column and one response column per model
  -> blinded benchmark.json for raters
  -> hidden model_map.json for admins
  -> Postgres seeded assignment slots
  -> clinician ratings
  -> admin CSV export
  -> external statistical analysis

The public repo is strongest in the middle of this chain: organizing inputs and outputs, calling APIs, handling retries/checkpoints, scoring HealthBench, and collecting blinded clinician ratings. It is weaker at the boundaries: raw MedQA transformation, proprietary browser collection, private RCQ release, and manuscript statistics.

Python Benchmark Pipeline

The main Python file is clinical_tools_extract/evaluation_pipeline.py.

Dependencies

The Python requirements are minimal:

  • openai
  • python-dotenv
  • tqdm
  • openpyxl
  • anthropic

The code uses the OpenAI SDK directly, optionally imports Anthropic, and calls the Google Generative Language REST API with urllib.

The pipeline expects API keys through environment variables:

  • OPENAI_API_KEY
  • ANTHROPIC_API_KEY

HealthBench grading has retry and timeout knobs:

  • HEALTHBENCH_GRADER_BACKOFF
  • HEALTHBENCH_GRADER_MAX_SLEEP_S
  • HEALTHBENCH_GRADER_TIMEOUT_S

Model Constants

The pipeline defines article-like model IDs:

  • openai-gpt-5-2
  • google-gemini-3-1-pro-preview
  • anthropic-claude-4-6-opus
  • openevidence-web
  • uptodate-ai
  • google-ai-overview

The default matrix for MedQA and HealthBench includes five models: the three frontier models plus OpenEvidence and UpToDate. Google AI Overview is included in the RCQ-style benchmark CSV model list, not the default MedQA/HealthBench matrix.

This matches the article design: Google AI Overview is treated as a real-world search-embedded comparator for RCQ, not as a MedQA/HealthBench model in the public benchmark matrix.

Input Loaders

The pipeline can load:

  • JSON arrays.
  • JSONL files.
  • XLSX files.

It has helpers for normalizing Excel cells, detecting likely question/id columns, writing JSONL, slugifying models and benchmark names, and resolving paths relative to the current working directory or script root.

This is useful because the pipeline is meant to bridge several data shapes:

  • Public benchmark JSONL.
  • Imported browser-collected outputs.
  • Local CSV/XLSX response matrices.
  • Per-model raw run files.

Model Registry Logic

The model registry is the abstraction that lets the same generate command handle very different systems.

The resolver can map a requested model into:

  • openai_api
  • anthropic_api
  • google_api
  • import_from_source
  • manual_only

If a model is OpenAI-like and not in the registry, the code assumes it can call OpenAI. If a model is one of the clinical/browser-only systems, the code either requires import_from_source through a registry entry or raises an error telling the user that automated answer retrieval is unavailable.

This is an important design choice. The public repo intentionally does not automate browser scraping for OpenEvidence or UpToDate. That reduces legal, ethical, and maintenance risk, but it means the public repo cannot regenerate those results by itself.

Generation Path

The generate_answers function is the central model-answer generation function.

It supports:

  • Parallel generation with ThreadPoolExecutor.
  • --max-workers.
  • --limit.
  • --resume by id key.
  • Output checkpoints every N completed rows.
  • Optional error rows through --allow-error-rows.
  • Run metadata written next to the output.
  • Search-enabled generation for supported providers.
  • Fallbacks when providers reject parameters such as temperature, top_p, or seed.

Provider-specific behavior:

  • OpenAI uses the Responses API when search is enabled and Chat Completions otherwise.
  • Anthropic uses the Messages API and, when search is enabled, a web_search_20250305 tool payload.
  • Google uses the Generative Language REST endpoint and adds google_search or google_search_retrieval tool payloads depending on model family.

The article says frontier model generations used deterministic parameters, search enabled, temperature 0.0, and seed 62 where supported. The code implements those defaults but also warns where the seed is ignored, such as OpenAI search through the Responses API and Anthropic.

Search Fallbacks

If search-enabled generation fails, the code attempts a no-search fallback and records fallback events in run metadata.

This is pragmatic for finishing runs, but it is analytically important. If some rows are answered with search and others are answered without search, the resulting benchmark is no longer a uniform "search-enabled" condition. The metadata records fallback attempts, successes, and failures, so a rigorous analysis should report whether fallbacks occurred and whether they were concentrated in any provider/model.

Run Metadata

For generation tasks, the pipeline writes a latest .run.json and timestamped run metadata file. The metadata includes:

  • Inputs and outputs.
  • Model requested.
  • Resolved provider and API model.
  • Generation parameters.
  • Runtime stats.
  • Search fallback stats.
  • Whether API keys were set.

This is a strong reproducibility feature. It does not make external APIs deterministic, but it records enough context to understand what was attempted.

MedQA Analysis

Dataset Shape

The raw Hugging Face MedQA rows have:

  • question
  • answer
  • options
  • meta_info
  • answer_idx
  • metamap_phrases

The options are a four-choice object with A, B, C, and D.

Pipeline Expectation

The public scoring function process_medqa expects a different shape:

  • question must contain a section marker ## Choices.
  • question must also contain ## Instructions.
  • The correct answer is expected in a key named correct.
  • The model answer is expected in answer.

This means raw Hugging Face MedQA cannot be passed directly into process_medqa without transformation. A prepared file such as data/benchmarks/medqa_500.jsonl is referenced in the README example, but the public CLI does not expose a prepare-medqa command analogous to prepare-healthbench.

This is one of the most important technical reproducibility gaps in the public repository. The article says the subset can be reproduced with seed 62, and the raw dataset is public, but the exact prompt format and transformation into the pipeline's expected fields are not fully encoded in an obvious public command.

Scoring Design

MedQA scoring uses two extractors:

  1. Regex answer extraction via MultiChoiceFilter.
  2. LLM extraction using GPT-4.1.

The LLM extraction prompt asks for a single answer letter. The extracted letter is compared with the row's correct key. Regex agreement is reported as a consistency check.

The output row receives:

  • RegEx_answer
  • LLM_extract
  • correctness

The scoring output reports:

  • Regex/LLM agreement excluding invalid answers.
  • Overall accuracy counting invalids as wrong.
  • Accuracy excluding invalids.

The article says GPT-4.1 extracted final answers and regex extraction was used in parallel as a consistency check, with disagreements manually verified. The code aligns with that general method.

Technical Notes

MedQA is a narrow benchmark. It rewards answer selection, not bedside usefulness. It can show that a model has strong medical exam knowledge, but it does not test whether the model communicates well, refuses appropriately, cites sources, handles ambiguous patient context, or integrates with clinical workflow.

From a code perspective, MedQA is also the path with the most obvious missing public preparation step. A rigorous reproduction should add a documented transformer:

Hugging Face MedQA row
  question + options + answer_idx
  -> sampled 500 rows with seed 62
  -> prompt string containing ## Choices and ## Instructions
  -> correct = answer_idx
  -> id = stable row id

Without that, two independent users may generate different prompt text even if they sample the same rows.

HealthBench Analysis

Dataset Shape

HealthBench rows expose:

  • prompt: chat-message list.
  • prompt_id: UUID-like identifier.
  • rubrics: list of scored criteria.
  • example_tags: theme/category tags.
  • canary: identifier string.

Rubric entries include:

  • criterion
  • points
  • tags

The first-row metadata shows both positive and negative rubric points. Positive points reward desired behavior. Negative points penalize undesirable behavior when the criterion is met.

Preparation

The CLI includes prepare-healthbench.

The function prepare_healthbench_questions:

  • Loads the source dataset.
  • Keeps only rows where prompt is exactly one user message.
  • Samples up to limit rows with random seed 62.
  • Writes JSONL rows with id, question, prompt_id, and rubrics.

This matches the article's description of sampling 500 single-turn prompts from HealthBench.

Grading Prompt

The pipeline includes a HealthBench grader template that asks the judge model to evaluate a conversation and rubric item, returning JSON with:

  • explanation
  • criteria_met

The prompt explicitly handles negative criteria: if a criterion is undesirable, criteria_met=true means the undesirable criterion is present. The scoring code then applies the criterion's point value, which may be negative.

This is a subtle but important design. A negative criterion should not be inverted by the judge. The judge answers whether the criterion is met; the point sign determines whether meeting it helps or hurts.

Single Judge vs Panel

HealthBench can run in two modes:

  • Single grading model.
  • Panel majority.

The default panel models are:

  • Claude Opus 4.6
  • Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview
  • GPT-5.2

This aligns with the article's method of using a three-model judge panel to reduce single-model bias.

The panel logic:

  • Calls judge models concurrently per criterion.
  • Uses majority vote among available judge results.
  • Defaults ties to criteria_met=false.
  • If all judge calls fail, defaults criteria_met=false.
  • Records panel votes and panel failures.

This failure policy is operationally robust but analytically asymmetric. For positive criteria, a failure-to-false default withholds points. For negative criteria, a failure-to-false default avoids penalty. The report or downstream analysis should track panel failure rates because failures can influence final scores.

Score Construction

For each criterion:

points_awarded = points if criteria_met else 0

For each row:

point_total = sum(points_awarded)
point_possible = sum(positive points only)

The final HealthBench score is:

grand_total / grand_possible

Because negative points can reduce grand_total, the score can reflect penalties without increasing the denominator.

Rerun Helper

The script rerun_failed_healthbench.py patches already-scored HealthBench files in place. It identifies rubric scores with failed panel votes, reruns those criteria, replaces the score entries, and recomputes totals.

This is useful for recovering from transient API failures. It should be used carefully because it mutates scored.json files in place. A reproducible workflow should keep backups or use versioned output paths before rerunning failed criteria.

Real Clinical Queries And The Review Platform

The public RCQ data is not available, but the platform that supports RCQ-style blinded review is public.

Benchmark Bundle Builder

The script clinical_eval_platform/scripts/build-benchmark.mjs converts a local query_responses.csv or query_responses.xlsx into blinded app data.

It detects:

  • An optional id/index column.
  • A required question/query/prompt column.
  • One response column per model.

It writes:

  • public/benchmark.json: rater-facing benchmark bundle.
  • data/model_map.json: admin-only model ID to true model name map.
  • data/benchmark_questions.json: question id index for assignment seeding.

The script anonymizes models as Model_A, Model_B, etc. The benchmarkId is the first 16 hex characters of the SHA-256 hash of the source file bytes. That makes the benchmark bundle stable for a given source file and changes when the source matrix changes.

The generated files are intentionally ignored because they may contain private clinical queries or model outputs.

Rater Entry

The landing page collects:

  • Rater name.
  • Optional access code.

The session API:

  • Normalizes names.
  • Enforces max name length of 80.
  • Checks EVAL_ACCESS_CODE if set.
  • Uses a Postgres advisory lock to avoid duplicate rows for the same name under concurrent login.
  • Reuses an existing rater row by case-insensitive name, preferring the row with existing work.

This is a simple and practical identity model for a controlled rater study. It is not a strong authentication system.

Database Schema

The schema is created lazily by ensureSchema.

Core tables:

  • raters
  • evaluations
  • benchmark_models
  • benchmark_state
  • response_review_slots
  • question_review_slots

The key evaluation table stores:

  • rater_id
  • benchmark_id
  • question_id
  • model_key
  • model_order
  • Four 1-4 Likert dimensions:
  • accuracy
  • completeness
  • safety
  • communication
  • Binary flags:
  • harmful
  • hallucinated
  • notes
  • is_complete

The schema seeds three response review slots per (benchmark_id, question_id, model_key). That supports the article's design of three independent clinician ratings per model response.

Assignment Logic

The assignment API:

  • Returns existing assignments for a rater.
  • Provides remaining-slot stats.
  • Claims a configurable number of response items, default 150.
  • Randomizes item selection.
  • Assigns at most one slot per (question_id, model_key) per claim.
  • Avoids assigning the same rater to the same response twice.
  • Only steals assignments from other raters if no unassigned slots remain and the existing assignee has not started.

This is more sophisticated than a simple random sample. It tries to balance study completion against avoiding duplicate rater work.

Rater UI

The evaluation client:

  • Loads benchmark.json.
  • Loads or claims assignments.
  • Randomizes question order per rater.
  • Randomizes model-response display order per rater and question.
  • Tracks completion.
  • Saves ratings through the evaluations API.
  • Supports offline-style full-benchmark viewing if assignment lookup fails because the database is unavailable.

The rating axes match the article:

  • Clinical correctness.
  • Safety/harm avoidance.
  • Completeness.
  • Clarity for clinicians.

The binary flags match the article:

  • Potentially harmful recommendation present?
  • Hallucinated facts?

Evaluation Save Logic

The evaluations API validates:

  • sessionId must be a UUID.
  • Likert ratings must be integers 1-4.
  • Binary flags must be true, false, or null.
  • Notes must be at most 4,000 characters.
  • A response must be assigned to the rater unless the benchmark has no seeded slots.

It upserts the evaluation row and sets is_complete=true only when all four Likert axes and both binary flags are present.

Admin And Export

The admin page displays:

  • Benchmarks.
  • Rater progress.
  • Model mapping.
  • Assignment management components.
  • Stats components.
  • Reset controls.

The CSV export route returns:

  • Rater name.
  • Benchmark id.
  • Question id.
  • Blinded model key.
  • True model name if available.
  • Model order.
  • Four rating dimensions.
  • Harm/hallucination flags.
  • Notes.
  • Completion state.
  • Timestamps.

This is the right shape for downstream statistical analysis.

Security Caveat

The local clone contains clinical_eval_platform/proxy.js, which protects /admin/:path* and /api/admin/:path* with HTTP Basic Auth when ADMIN_PASSWORD is set. This resolves the earlier connector-only uncertainty about whether the admin page and export route are protected.

The security conclusion is therefore narrower and more practical:

  • If ADMIN_PASSWORD is set and the deployment platform runs Next's proxy layer correctly, /admin and /api/admin/* should be protected.
  • If ADMIN_PASSWORD is blank, admin pages and exports are intentionally open.
  • Before using real rater names, notes, clinical queries, or model mappings, verify the deployed app actually applies proxy.js to the export route and admin page.

This still matters because the export route includes rater names, notes, benchmark IDs, question IDs, and model mappings. If benchmark questions are de-identified clinical queries, export exposure could become a privacy and governance issue.

Important Technical Findings

1. The repository matches the article's high-level workflow

The code is not a toy example. It has real support for multi-provider generation, deterministic run parameters, per-row checkpointing, HealthBench rubric scoring, panel judging, blinded rater assignments, and admin export.

For a research codebase, the separation is reasonable:

  • Python for benchmark generation/scoring.
  • Next.js for clinician review.
  • Postgres for rater state.
  • Local files for private benchmark bundles.

2. It is not a complete reproduction package

The public repo does not include:

  • The RCQ dataset.
  • The manually collected browser outputs.
  • A visible MedQA preparation command.
  • Article-level statistical scripts.
  • Raw generated outputs used for the paper.
  • A frozen dependency lockfile for Python.
  • A guaranteed way to access historical model versions.

That does not make the code invalid. It means the code should be described as "supporting code" and "workflow code," not a complete deterministic reproduction artifact.

3. MedQA preparation needs documentation

The article says the MedQA 500-item subset is reproducible with seed 62, but the public pipeline's MedQA scorer expects prepared prompts, not raw Hugging Face rows. The report should not imply that a user can point process_medqa directly at the Hugging Face dataset.

A robust reproduction should add:

  • A prepare-medqa subcommand.
  • Exact seed behavior.
  • Exact split choice.
  • Exact prompt template.
  • correct = answer_idx.
  • Stable row ids.

4. Manual model imports are central, not optional

OpenEvidence, UpToDate Expert AI, and Google AI Overview are not API-driven in this repo. The code treats them as manual/imported outputs. Any reproduction that omits the exact manually collected outputs cannot reproduce the clinical-tool comparison.

This also means output formatting is not normalized. The article intentionally preserves each system's interface-like answer style because clarity and usability are part of the evaluation.

5. HealthBench grading is strong but not neutral

The panel design is better than relying on one judge model, but it still uses frontier LLMs to judge frontier LLMs and clinical tools. The article explicitly acknowledges this limitation.

Code-level concerns:

  • Majority among available votes can become a two-model or one-model decision if judge calls fail.
  • Ties default to false.
  • All failures default to false.
  • False default benefits negative criteria by avoiding penalties.
  • Failures are recorded, but downstream summaries must actually inspect them.

6. Search fallback can change experimental conditions

The generation code may fall back from search-enabled generation to no-search generation. This is useful for operational completion, but if it happens in a benchmark run, rows are not all generated under the same condition.

A rigorous analysis should include:

  • Number of search fallback attempts by model.
  • Number of fallback successes and failures.
  • Whether fallback rows were excluded, retained, or sensitivity-tested.

7. The clinician platform's assignment model is thoughtful

The app does more than present random rows. It seeds response-level slots, claims work per rater, avoids duplicates, and randomizes response order per rater. That supports blinded review and reduces ordering bias.

The platform also keeps a model map separate from rater-facing benchmark data, which is the right blinding architecture.

8. Admin security should be tightened before real deployment

The inspected code shows inconsistent admin authentication coverage. For internal research use behind a protected network, this may be handled externally. For internet deployment, it is not enough to rely on an environment variable if not all admin routes use it.

Specific route to review first:

  • clinical_eval_platform/app/api/admin/export/route.js

9. The public repository does not appear to implement manuscript statistics

The article reports McNemar tests, Wilcoxon signed-rank tests, Nemenyi tests, cumulative link models, mixed models, Krippendorff's alpha, PABAK, Cochran's Q, Fisher's exact tests, and bootstrap confidence intervals.

The inspected public code does not appear to implement that statistical analysis layer. It produces scored data and CSV exports that can feed such an analysis, but the final manuscript statistics appear to be outside the visible code paths.

This is important for technical readers: running evaluate-matrix is not the same as recreating Figure 2 or Extended Data Figure 3.

Non-Technical Interpretation

For clinicians

The study suggests that specialized clinical AI tools should not be assumed to be better simply because they are marketed for medicine. In the tested tasks, the frontier LLMs performed better on exam-style questions, rubric-scored health responses, and blinded clinician ratings of real clinical queries.

However, this does not mean clinicians should use frontier LLMs without safeguards. The study does not assess all clinical workflows, does not test live patient-care integration, does not evaluate citation reliability, and does not replace local clinical governance.

Clinicians should read this as evidence that independent evaluation matters. A model's interface, institution, pricing, or clinical branding is not enough.

For health system leaders

The procurement implication is direct: demand head-to-head evidence before adopting or renewing clinical AI tools. Require local validation on your own tasks and users. Include frontline clinicians in blinded review. Measure safety, completeness, clarity, refusal behavior, latency, citation quality, and workflow fit.

A specialized tool may still be worth buying if it integrates well, cites sources reliably, handles governance, supports audit logs, protects data, and reduces liability. But those are separate claims from raw answer quality. They need their own evidence.

For compliance and governance teams

The RCQ dataset is private for a reason. Even de-identified clinician queries can be sensitive. If this platform is adapted locally, generated benchmark bundles and exported CSVs should be treated as potentially sensitive research data.

Before deployment:

  • Protect admin routes.
  • Protect exports.
  • Avoid public hosting of real query text.
  • Verify database access controls.
  • Establish data retention rules.
  • Review IRB or quality-improvement requirements.
  • Log model versions and collection dates.

For technical teams

The repository is useful as a benchmark scaffold. It is not enough by itself to certify a model or reproduce the paper. Engineering teams should harden it before production-like use:

  • Add lockfiles.
  • Add schema migrations instead of lazy schema creation if deployed long-term.
  • Add tests around scoring edge cases.
  • Add explicit MedQA preparation.
  • Add stronger auth for admin/export.
  • Add dataset pinning.
  • Add statistical notebooks.
  • Add structured provenance for imported browser outputs.

Technical Appendix: File-By-File Notes

README.md

The README is accurate as an orientation layer. It describes the two code areas, gives installation examples, and states that OpenEvidence, UpToDate Expert AI, and Google Search AI Overview were collected outside the public repo and should be imported as pre-collected outputs through data/runs/model_registry.json.

The README is also the clearest public statement that the repository is not trying to automate browser extraction for proprietary clinical systems.

clinical_tools_extract/evaluation_pipeline.py

This is the central benchmark file. It contains:

  • Constants for model IDs, default panel models, temperature, seed, retries, and timeouts.
  • Input loaders for JSON, JSONL, and XLSX.
  • Model-registry resolution.
  • HealthBench preparation.
  • Multi-provider model generation.
  • MedQA answer extraction and scoring.
  • HealthBench rubric scoring.
  • Matrix status and matrix evaluation.
  • Benchmark CSV generation for the blinded review app.
  • CLI parser and command dispatch.

Key subcommands:

  • prepare-healthbench
  • generate
  • generate-benchmark-csv
  • medqa
  • healthbench
  • matrix-status
  • evaluate-matrix

The file is intentionally broad. For long-term maintainability, it would benefit from being split into modules such as io.py, providers.py, medqa.py, healthbench.py, matrix.py, and cli.py. That is not required for reproducing the paper, but it would make testing easier.

clinical_tools_extract/rerun_failed_healthbench.py

This helper reruns only HealthBench criteria where panel grading produced failures. It loads existing scored.json, identifies failed criteria, calls the panel scorer again, replaces those criterion scores, and recomputes totals.

Because it writes the scored file in place, a rigorous workflow should copy or version the scored file before using this script.

clinical_tools_extract/requirements.txt

The dependency list is intentionally small but not pinned by version. For exact reproducibility, a lockfile or frozen pip freeze output should be captured at run time.

clinical_tools_extract/.env.example

This file documents the required provider API keys and HealthBench grader retry settings. It does not include any model registry example, which would be useful for users importing clinical tool outputs.

clinical_eval_platform/scripts/build-benchmark.mjs

This script is the bridge between a local model-response matrix and the blinded web app. It:

  • Reads query_responses.csv or legacy query_responses.xlsx.
  • Detects id and question columns.
  • Treats all other non-empty columns as model response columns.
  • Generates stable benchmark IDs from the source file hash.
  • Writes a blinded public benchmark JSON.
  • Writes a hidden model map.
  • Writes a benchmark question index for assignment seeding.

This is one of the cleanest pieces of the codebase because it enforces the blinding boundary.

clinical_eval_platform/app/page.jsx

This is the rater landing page. It stores session information in local storage, supports optional access codes, and lets a returning rater resume or switch identity.

The identity model is suitable for a controlled research study but not for strong user authentication.

clinical_eval_platform/app/eval/EvalClient.jsx

This is the main rater experience. It:

  • Loads public/benchmark.json.
  • Loads or claims assignments.
  • Randomizes question order and response order per rater.
  • Tracks completion.
  • Saves ratings.
  • Supports auto-advance.
  • Falls back to showing all benchmark items if assignment lookup fails because the database is unavailable.

The deterministic per-rater shuffling is a good design choice because it reduces presentation bias while remaining stable across browser refreshes.

clinical_eval_platform/app/api/session/route.js

This route creates or resumes rater sessions. It normalizes names, checks optional access code, prevents duplicate rows under concurrent logins using an advisory lock, and reuses an existing rater row by name.

clinical_eval_platform/app/api/assignments/route.js

This route manages response-level assignment slots. It is central to the three-raters-per-response design. Its "steal only if no unassigned slots remain and current assignee has not started" rule is a reasonable compromise between study completion and fairness.

clinical_eval_platform/app/api/evaluations/route.js

This route loads and saves rater evaluations. It validates all rating values, gates writes to assigned items when slots exist, upserts rows, and marks a row complete only when all required axes and binary flags are filled.

clinical_eval_platform/lib/server/schema.js

This file lazily creates all Postgres tables and seeds model mappings and review slots. Lazy schema creation is convenient for a research app, but a production deployment should consider explicit migrations.

clinical_eval_platform/lib/server/db.js

This file resolves the Postgres connection string from common environment variables and creates a small connection pool. In production it requires SSL.

clinical_eval_platform/app/admin/page.jsx

The admin page renders benchmark progress, rater progress, model mapping, stats, assignment management, rater management, and reset controls. The page itself does not implement auth checks, but local proxy.js protects /admin/:path* when ADMIN_PASSWORD is set.

clinical_eval_platform/app/api/admin/export/route.js

This route exports clinician ratings as CSV. It includes rater names, model mapping, notes, flags, and timestamps. The route itself does not implement auth checks, but local proxy.js protects /api/admin/:path* when ADMIN_PASSWORD is set.

This route should still be verified in the deployed environment because it is the most sensitive egress path.

clinical_eval_platform/proxy.js

This file applies HTTP Basic Auth to /admin/:path* and /api/admin/:path* when ADMIN_PASSWORD is configured. If ADMIN_PASSWORD is blank, the proxy intentionally allows access.

clinical_eval_platform/app/api/admin/stats/route.js

This route computes admin dashboard metrics and does include an admin Basic Auth check when ADMIN_PASSWORD is set.

clinical_eval_platform/app/api/admin/reset/route.js

This route resets benchmark evaluations and assignments. It includes admin Basic Auth and confirmation strings such as NUKE or NUKE ALL.

clinical_eval_platform/app/api/admin/raters/route.js

This route manages raters and includes admin Basic Auth. Deleting a rater also deletes their evaluations and clears their assignments.

clinical_eval_platform/.env.example

The platform expects a Postgres URL and optionally supports:

  • EVAL_ACCESS_CODE
  • NEXT_PUBLIC_DEFAULT_ASSIGNMENT_COUNT
  • ADMIN_USER
  • ADMIN_PASSWORD

The file's admin protection comment should be reconciled with the actual route-level checks before deployment.

Technical Appendix: Glossary

  • API model: The provider-specific model name actually sent to OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google.
  • Benchmark matrix: The set of model-by-benchmark raw output files under data/runs.
  • Blinding: Hiding true model identity from clinician raters by using labels such as Model_A.
  • Clinical correctness: Whether the answer is medically accurate and nuanced.
  • Completeness: Whether the answer covers the important clinical content.
  • Frontier LLM: A general-purpose state-of-the-art model from a major model provider.
  • HealthBench: A rubric-based benchmark for health assistant responses.
  • LLM-as-judge: Use of a model to score another model's output against criteria.
  • MedQA: A multiple-choice USMLE-style medical exam benchmark.
  • Model registry: Local JSON that maps model IDs to provider modes, API model names, and source files.
  • RCQ: Real Clinical Queries, the article's private benchmark derived from clinician use.
  • RAG: Retrieval-augmented generation, where a model retrieves external documents before answering.
  • Refusal: A model response that declines to answer rather than providing substantive content.
  • Rubric: A list of criteria and points used to score an answer.
  • Search-enabled generation: Model generation with provider search tools enabled.

Bottom-Line Assessment

The article makes a strong and timely argument: specialized clinical AI tools should be independently benchmarked against frontier general-purpose LLMs before institutions assume they are better. The public codebase supports much of that evaluation workflow, especially HealthBench scoring and blinded clinician review, but it should not be mistaken for a full reproduction artifact.

The most technically valuable part of the code is the architecture: generation/import separated from scoring, scoring separated from blinded clinician review, and review exports separated from downstream statistics. The most important reproducibility gaps are external to the core architecture: private RCQ data, manual browser-collected outputs, missing MedQA preparation, and missing manuscript statistics scripts.

For a mixed technical and non-technical audience, the key message is this: the study is not a permission slip to deploy frontier LLMs freely in medicine. It is evidence that clinical AI tools need independent, transparent, head-to-head evaluation, and that general-purpose LLMs may outperform specialized tools on many clinically relevant benchmark tasks.
